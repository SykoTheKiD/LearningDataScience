How to Interpret the Graphs¶

Pairwise Plot¶

The pairwise plot is a scatter plot plotted against each feature. Scatter plots tell us the relationship between two variables. If you cannot see any positive or negative relationship between two feature then the two features are not related to each other.

Some things we can notice are

  • Fiber and potassium are positively correlated
  • As sugar increases the rating goes down, suggesting that the survey audience might be adults
  • Sodium and carbohydrates are also positively correlated

Correlation Matrix¶

A correlation gives us a much more precise measure as to how related (positive/negative) two feature are to each other.

The Pearson Correaltion value can be interpretted as follows:

Exactly –1. A perfect downhill (negative) linear relationship

–0.70. A strong downhill (negative) linear relationship

–0.50. A moderate downhill (negative) relationship

–0.30. A weak downhill (negative) linear relationship

  1. No linear relationship

+0.30. A weak uphill (positive) linear relationship

+0.50. A moderate uphill (positive) relationship

+0.70. A strong uphill (positive) linear relationship

Exactly +1. A perfect uphill (positive) linear relationship

Understanding the data distribution¶

Another important part of understanding data is to check the distribution of the data. We would like the data to all the as close to standard normal as possible.

In [8]:
sns.distplot(cereal_frame['rating'])
plt.show()

Looking at the ratings it is skewed to a a bit left. To normalize this data we can use box-cox transforms.

In [9]:
from scipy.stats import boxcox

sns.distplot(boxcox(cereal_frame['rating'], lmbda=0.0))
plt.show()

Now we can see that the distrobution is normal. Let's try this with other features in the dataset.
